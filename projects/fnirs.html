Summary

Neurofeedback training is a promising rehabilitation technique for patients with degraded motor function. Existing fNIRS systems capable of supporting it are expensive, research-oriented, and inaccessible to front-line rehabilitation clinics. In collaboration with Western University PhD candidate Fraser MacRae, my team and I designed and built a low-cost fNIRS-based neurofeedback prototype for clinical applications from the ground up.

Technical Design

The system integrates a wearable cap with 740nm and 850nm NIR emitters and OPT101 photodiode detectors, an ESP32 microcontroller with custom firmware for LED timing and ADC sampling, signal processing completed by utilizing the modified Beer-Lambert Law, and a dual-interface software application: one for clinicians to manage sessions, and one featuring four interactive neurofeedback games for patients.

Technical Challenges

Getting a clear signal by eliminating noise from the scalp, hair, and ambient light was a big challenge. Implementing hardware filters, as well as a scalp coupling index (short channel subtraction) in the signal processing improved the signal quality. Additionally, sourcing powerful near-IR LEDs strong enough to produce light that can penetrate the skull was a challenge.
